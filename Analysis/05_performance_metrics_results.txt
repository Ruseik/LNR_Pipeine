In the performance evaluation phase, discrimination of the candidate models was assessed using Uno’s C-statistic with truncation at the 75th percentile of observed event times (895 days). Model A demonstrated a C-statistic of 0.4248 (95% CI: 0.3418–0.5078), while Model B showed a slightly higher discrimination with a C-statistic of 0.4667 (95% CI: 0.3637–0.5698). Despite this numerical improvement, both models exhibited limited discriminative ability, with confidence intervals overlapping substantially and including values close to 0.5, indicating poor-to-modest predictive performance.

The primary planned comparison between multivariable models (Model C vs Model D) could not be conducted due to an inadequate number of outcome events (<80 events), precluding reliable multivariable analysis. Consequently, a hypothesis-generating comparison was performed at Layer 1 between the unadjusted models. The absolute difference in C-statistic between Model B and Model A (ΔC) was 0.0419, suggesting a small incremental improvement in discrimination with Model B; however, this difference should be interpreted cautiously given the limited sample size and lack of statistical validation.

Decision-curve analysis was not performed in accordance with the prespecified analytic framework, as the dataset did not meet the minimum event threshold required for clinically meaningful net benefit estimation.

Model fit statistics favored Model A over Model B. Specifically, Model A demonstrated lower Akaike Information Criterion (AIC: 249.4 vs 252.6) and Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC: 251.5 vs 254.7), indicating a better balance of model fit and parsimony.

Overall, under conditions of limited event counts, neither model demonstrated adequate discriminative performance, and although Model B showed a marginal increase in C-statistic, Model A was preferred based on information-theoretic criteria. These findings are considered exploratory and hypothesis-generating, and require validation in a larger cohort with sufficient event rates.
