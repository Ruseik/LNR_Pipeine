Of 137 records in the database, 75 were excluded because they were node-negative, while no cases were excluded for invalid lymph node data, missing survival data, or missing/zero total lymph node examination (TLE). The final analytic cohort therefore comprised 62 node-positive colorectal cancer patients. Within this cohort, there were 34 overall survival (OS) events and 24 disease-specific survival (DSS) events, placing the dataset in the “inadequate” event tier for higher-order modeling. The median TLE was 15 nodes; 25.8% of patients had TLE below the AJCC minimum of 12 nodes, and 12.9% had TLE below 8 nodes. The median lymph node ratio (LNR) was 0.133. By LNR tier, 7 patients were classified as low risk (<0.05), 29 as intermediate risk (0.05–0.20), and 26 as high risk (>0.20).

In the unadjusted discrimination analysis, the N-stage model showed a concordance statistic (C-statistic) of 0.4248 (95% CI 0.3418–0.5078), whereas the LNR model showed a higher C-statistic of 0.4667 (95% CI 0.3637–0.5698), corresponding to an absolute improvement in discrimination of 0.0419 in favor of LNR. Reclassification analysis further demonstrated discordance between conventional nodal stage and LNR-based risk grouping. Among the 41 patients with N1 disease, 7 were classified as low LNR risk, 27 as intermediate risk, and 7 as high risk. Among the 21 patients with N2 disease, none were classified as low risk, 2 were intermediate risk, and 19 were high risk. This pattern indicated that LNR identified risk heterogeneity within nodal stages, particularly by separating a substantial proportion of N1 cases into intermediate- and high-risk groups.

Kaplan–Meier survival curves were generated for OS and DSS according to both N-stage and LNR tier, and cumulative incidence functions were produced for cancer-specific survival. Composite survival figures and sensitivity analyses were also compiled to support the primary prognostic comparison.
