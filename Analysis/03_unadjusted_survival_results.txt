In the analytic cohort of 62 patients, there were 34 overall survival (OS) events, and the dataset was classified as an inadequate analytic tier because fewer than 80 events were available.

On Kaplan–Meier analysis, OS differed significantly by N stage. Patients with N1 disease had a median OS of 3067 days, whereas those with N2 disease had a median OS of 705 days. The log-rank test showed a statistically significant difference between the two groups (χ²=4.0, p=0.046). Landmark OS estimates for N1 versus N2 were 84.7% versus 61.2% at 1 year, 56.5% versus 40.8% at 3 years, and 53.9% versus 30.6% at 5 years, respectively.

By contrast, OS did not differ meaningfully across lymph node ratio (LNR) tiers. Median OS was 1131 days in the low LNR group, 1380 days in the intermediate LNR group, and 1060 days in the high LNR group. The overall log-rank test was not significant (χ²=0.2, p=0.918), and pairwise log-rank testing with Bonferroni correction did not identify any significant differences between LNR tiers. Landmark OS estimates at 1, 3, and 5 years were 85.7%, 57.1%, and 42.9% for low LNR; 78.0%, 52.0%, and 48.3% for intermediate LNR; and 72.7%, 48.5%, and 44.4% for high LNR, respectively.

Restricted mean survival time (RMST) was planned at 5 years, but the procedure returned missing values for all subgroups, so no interpretable RMST estimates were obtained from this output.

In univariate Cox proportional hazards regression, N2 stage was associated with worse OS compared with N1 stage (hazard ratio [HR] 1.97, 95% confidence interval [CI] 1.00–3.89; p=0.0496). Continuous LNR was not significantly associated with OS in the linear model (HR 1.41, 95% CI 0.55–3.63; p=0.473). When LNR was modeled categorically, neither the intermediate LNR group (HR 1.00, 95% CI 0.33–3.05; p=0.993) nor the high LNR group (HR 1.16, 95% CI 0.38–3.51; p=0.794) differed significantly from the reference low LNR group. Among the other univariate covariates, lymphovascular invasion was associated with poorer OS (HR 2.04, 95% CI 1.03–4.04; p=0.042), and adequate total lymph node evaluation (≥12 nodes) was associated with improved OS (HR 0.48, 95% CI 0.24–0.96; p=0.0385); grade showed a borderline association (HR 2.37, 95% CI 0.90–6.23; p=0.0797). Age, sex, T stage group, and total lymph node count were not significantly associated with OS in these univariate analyses.

For disease-specific survival, 24 DSS events were observed, and Kaplan–Meier analyses were generated for both N stage and LNR tier.
