The statistical analysis was conducted in R using a pre-specified, reproducible workflow for node-positive colorectal cancer survival analyses . The cohort was restricted to patients with node-positive disease, defined in the primary analysis by at least one positive lymph node, and lymph node ratio (LNR) was analyzed both as a continuous variable and as a categorical variable using Rosenberg cut points of <0.05, 0.05–0.20, and >0.20; sensitivity analyses considered alternative LNR thresholds and lymph node yield adequacy thresholds of 12 and 8 examined nodes . Baseline characteristics were summarized using descriptive statistics, and group imbalances were assessed with standardized mean differences. Overall survival was estimated using the Kaplan–Meier method and compared by the log-rank test. Prognostic associations were evaluated with univariable and multivariable Cox proportional hazards regression models, with proportional hazards assumptions assessed using Schoenfeld residuals. Where non-linearity was anticipated or supported by the data, restricted cubic splines were used to model continuous effects. Cause-specific survival was analyzed using the Fine–Gray subdistribution hazard model for competing risks. Missing data were handled with multiple imputation by chained equations when missingness exceeded the prespecified threshold. Model performance was quantified using Uno’s concordance statistic, with discrimination evaluated under truncation of follow-up time, and calibration was assessed with bootstrap resampling using the bias-corrected and accelerated method. Clinical utility was examined with decision curve analysis across a prespecified range of threshold probabilities. Additional prespecified analyses included restricted mean survival time, landmark analysis at 180 days, and bootstrap-based internal validation. All tests were two-sided, and statistical significance was defined as p < 0.05
