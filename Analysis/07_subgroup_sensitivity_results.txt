Pre-specified subgroup and sensitivity analyses were performed in the analytic cohort of 62 patients, among whom 34 overall survival events occurred. Because the cohort was below the threshold for formal interaction testing, all subgroup findings were interpreted as descriptive and exploratory.

In the primary subgroup analysis according to therapeutic lymph node examination (TLE) adequacy, 16 patients had inadequate TLE (<12 nodes) and 46 had adequate TLE (≥12 nodes). In the inadequate TLE subgroup, the concordance statistic was 0.4958 for the N-stage model and 0.3644 for the LNR model (ΔC = -0.1314). In the adequate TLE subgroup, the concordance statistic was 0.3875 for the N-stage model and 0.4412 for the LNR model (ΔC = 0.0537). Thus, LNR showed slightly better discrimination than N-stage in the adequate harvest subgroup, whereas N-stage performed better in the inadequate harvest subgroup. Formal testing for LNR × TLE adequacy interaction was not performed because the event threshold required for interaction analysis was not met.

In the T-stage subgroup analysis, 9 patients had T1/T2 disease and 53 had T3/T4 disease. The T1/T2 subgroup contained too few observations and events for reliable discrimination analysis. In the T3/T4 subgroup, the concordance statistic was 0.4230 for the N-stage model and 0.4470 for the LNR model (ΔC = 0.0240), indicating a small advantage for LNR.

In the nodal-stage subgroup analysis, LNR discrimination was assessed separately in N1 and N2 disease. The concordance statistic for LNR was 0.4478 in the N1 subgroup (N = 41, 19 events) and 0.5150 in the N2 subgroup (N = 21, 15 events), suggesting somewhat better discrimination in N2 disease.

A 6-month landmark analysis was conducted to reduce potential bias from early postoperative mortality. After excluding patients who experienced the event within 180 days, 51 patients remained with 25 events. In this landmark cohort, the N-stage Cox model yielded a hazard ratio of 1.78 for N2 versus N1 disease (95% CI 0.80–3.97; p = 0.158; concordance = 0.566), whereas the continuous LNR model yielded a hazard ratio of 1.45 per unit increase in LNR (95% CI 0.48–4.34; p = 0.506; concordance = 0.534). When modeled using concordance statistics, the landmark N-stage model had C = 0.4461 and the landmark LNR model had C = 0.4810.

Sensitivity analyses using alternative LNR cutpoints showed no meaningful improvement in prognostic separation. With the 0.05/0.40 ternary categorization, the distribution was 7 low, 37 intermediate, and 18 high, and the log-rank test was not significant (p = 0.648). Using a binary LNR threshold of 0.20, 36 patients were classified as low and 26 as high, again with no significant survival difference (p = 0.679). Concordance statistics for LNR modeling were similar across categorizations: 0.4793 for the original LNR tiers, 0.4638 for the 0.05/0.40 cutpoints, 0.4839 for the 0.20 binary threshold, and 0.4667 for continuous LNR.

Overall, these subgroup and sensitivity analyses showed that the prognostic performance of LNR relative to nodal stage was inconsistent across strata and did not demonstrate a stable superiority of one metric over the other in this small cohort.
