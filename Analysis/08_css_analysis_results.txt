Cancer-specific survival analysis was performed in the full analytic cohort of 62 patients, and cause-of-death data completeness was 100%, meeting the prespecified threshold for competing-risks analysis. During follow-up, 34 deaths occurred in total, including 24 cancer-specific deaths and 10 non-cancer deaths, while 28 patients were alive or censored. In the competing-risks framework, the cumulative incidence of cancer-specific death differed significantly by N stage (Gray’s test, p = 0.016), whereas the cumulative incidence of the competing non-cancer death did not differ by N stage (p = 0.709). In contrast, cumulative incidence did not differ significantly across LNR tiers for either cancer-specific death (p = 0.625) or non-cancer death (p = 0.478).

In Fine-Gray subdistribution hazard modeling, N2 stage was associated with a significantly higher risk of cancer-specific death compared with N1 stage (subdistribution hazard ratio [sHR] 2.64, 95% confidence interval [CI] 1.20–5.81; p = 0.015). LNR analyzed as a continuous variable was not significantly associated with cancer-specific death (sHR 1.78, 95% CI 0.64–4.94; p = 0.27). When LNR was modeled categorically, neither the intermediate tier (0.05–0.20; sHR 1.74, 95% CI 0.46–6.53; p = 0.41) nor the high tier (>0.20; sHR 2.03, 95% CI 0.54–7.71; p = 0.30) showed a statistically significant association with cancer-specific death compared with the low tier (<0.05).
