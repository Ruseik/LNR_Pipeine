## Critical Evaluation — Point-by-Point Assessment

### Point 1 — The estimand is blurred
**Validity: Fully valid. This is the most important critique.**

The original protocol genuinely does try to answer two structurally different questions simultaneously. The **prognostic question** asks: is LNR independently associated with survival? This requires a multivariable Cox model and produces hazard ratios. The **prediction-model question** asks: does replacing N-stage with LNR improve a model's ability to discriminate and calibrate survival? This requires C-statistic comparison, NRI, and decision-curve analysis. These questions have different target audiences — the prognostic question speaks to biologists and oncologists; the prediction-model question speaks to staging committees. Trying to answer both in one protocol without declaring a primary estimand means neither answer is authoritative.

**Revision:** The primary estimand must be **incremental predictive performance** — specifically, whether adding LNR to a base model containing established clinicopathological factors produces a clinically meaningful and statistically robust improvement in discrimination for OS. This is the estimand that directly speaks to the translational objective of influencing AJCC/RCPath staging frameworks, because staging systems are prediction tools, not association tables. The independent-association question (Cox HR for LNR) is retained as a secondary estimand — it provides biological plausibility for the primary finding, but it is not the primary claim.

### Point 2 — Over-reliance on categorical LNR cutpoints
**Validity: Fully valid. A significant statistical concern.**

Categorising a continuous variable at fixed thresholds is statistically lossy under any circumstances — it reduces the information content of the variable, inflates the apparent precision of threshold effects, and is inherently sample-dependent when thresholds are data-derived. For LNR specifically, the literature shows that cutpoints derived in Western cohorts (e.g. 0.05/0.20) may not transport faithfully to a South Asian population with different lymph node harvest distributions. If the median TLE in this cohort is lower, the LNR distribution will be right-shifted relative to Western series, meaning the 0.20 threshold may fall in a different quantile of the local distribution than it did in the Rosenberg derivation set.

**Revision:** Continuous LNR modelled with restricted cubic splines (3–4 knots, placed at default percentiles: 10th, 50th, 90th for 3 knots) becomes the **primary analytic form**. This preserves the full prognostic signal, allows detection of non-linearity in the LNR-mortality relationship, and produces a dose-response curve that is intrinsically more persuasive to staging reviewers than category contrasts. The pre-specified categorical tiers (Low/Intermediate/High using 0.05/0.20 thresholds) are retained as a **secondary presentation layer** — useful for clinical communication and for comparison with published literature, but not the primary basis of inference. An additional sensitivity analysis will examine the 0.05/0.40 binary system. The X-tile exploratory analysis is deprioritised further — it moves to an appendix-only finding clearly labelled as hypothesis-generating.

### Point 3 — Asymmetric model comparison
**Validity: Partially valid, but requires nuance. The critique is correct in direction but overstates the problem.**

The critique is correct that LNR and N-stage are not interchangeable variables — LNR is derived from its denominator (TLE), while N-stage is not. Therefore, adjusting for TLE in both models does not create a symmetrical comparison. When TLE is included in the LNR model, you are in effect adjusting out one of the components from which LNR is constructed, which can artificially deflate LNR's apparent contribution. However, not adjusting for TLE creates a different problem: any apparent advantage of LNR over N-stage may simply reflect TLE's own prognostic effect (independent of LNR), which would be a spurious attribution.

However, the critique slightly overstates the concern. The purpose of including TLE is not to adjust away LNR's mechanism — it is to ensure that any residual LNR advantage reflects ratio information (the proportion positive) rather than harvest volume alone. If LNR retains prognostic value after TLE adjustment, that is a stronger finding, not a weaker one.

**Revision:** The model comparison strategy is expanded into a **three-layer framework** rather than two models:

- **Layer 1 (Unadjusted):** N-stage only vs LNR only — raw discrimination without harvest adjustment. This answers: does LNR contain survival information, full stop?
- **Layer 2 (TLE-adjusted):** N-stage + TLE vs LNR + TLE — discrimination with harvest adequacy accounted for. This answers: does LNR add value beyond what harvest volume itself explains?
- **Layer 3 (Full MVA):** N-stage + TLE + all other covariates vs LNR + TLE + all other covariates — clinically realistic comparison. This is the primary model comparison for the main estimand.

The delta C-statistic is computed and reported at all three layers. This directly addresses the critique while also generating a more informative narrative: if LNR's advantage over N-stage diminishes substantially from Layer 1 to Layer 2, that tells us the LNR advantage was largely a harvest-volume artifact. If it persists into Layer 3, the case for LNR is robust.

### Point 4 — Over-parameterization without a hard event target
**Validity: Fully valid. This is a critical implementation gap.**

"EPV" framing is necessary but not sufficient. The original protocol specified EPV targets but did not convert these into a minimum event count for the specific model architecture being tested. The primary model (Model D in the revised framework) includes: LNR (as a spline — counts as 2–3 df, not 1), T-stage (3 levels = 2 df), grade (2 df), LVI (1 df), PNI (1 df), resection margin (1 df), TLE (continuous spline = 2–3 df), tumour site (1 df), and age (continuous, 1 df). That is approximately 13–16 degrees of freedom total. At EPV = 10, this requires **130–160 events minimum** for the full model. For the interaction test between LNR and TLE adequacy (SO5, H₄), the power requirement is roughly four times the main effect requirement — meaning the TLE subgroup analysis is likely underpowered in a single-centre series unless event counts exceed 200+.

The analytic plan is restructured so that model complexity scales to the achieved event count:The critical discipline here is that the event-count tier must be **assigned by a blinded statistician after counting events but before any outcome modelling**, and the permitted analysis list for that tier must not be exceeded. This prevents post-hoc escalation of model complexity when the data look promising.

### Point 5 — Competing risks for secondary endpoints
**Validity: Fully valid, practically important, often overlooked in surgical oncology literature.**

Standard Cox modelling for cause-specific survival treats deaths from non-cancer causes as simple censoring events. In a colorectal cancer cohort — particularly an older one or one spanning 15 years — non-cancer mortality is not negligible and is not non-informative censoring. Patients who die of cardiovascular disease cannot develop CRC recurrence; their censoring is therefore informative. The Fine-Gray subdistribution hazard model is the appropriate method for DFS (where the event is recurrence and death from other causes is a competing event) and CSS (where the event is cancer-specific death and non-cancer death is a competing event). For OS, standard Cox remains appropriate because there is no competing event — all deaths are the outcome.

**Revision:** The secondary endpoint analysis is restructured:

- **OS** (primary outcome): Standard Cox proportional hazards. This remains unchanged.
- **DFS** (secondary): Fine-Gray subdistribution hazard model as the primary analysis for DFS. Standard Cox as a sensitivity analysis. The subdistribution hazard ratio for LNR tier is the reported estimate.
- **CSS** (secondary): Fine-Gray model with non-cancer death as the competing event. Requires cause-of-death data — if this is unavailable from records (a realistic limitation in Sri Lankan retrospective data), CSS is dropped as a formal endpoint and replaced with a descriptive analysis of cause of death, with explicit acknowledgement of the limitation.

This revision is not merely methodological tidiness — it directly affects interpretation. If LNR predicts OS but not DFS under competing-risks analysis, this suggests LNR may partly capture comorbidity burden rather than tumour biology, which is a substantive finding in its own right.

### Point 6 — NRI over-promoted relative to DCA and calibration
**Validity: Fully valid and important for the translational objective specifically.**

This is a well-established methodological critique in the clinical prediction literature. NRI is mathematically unstable with modest sample sizes, its interpretation depends critically on how risk categories are defined (which is circular when the categories themselves are under investigation), and it can be positive even when the new model performs worse on absolute risk predictions. More importantly for the AJCC/RCPath audience, decision-curve analysis is far more persuasive because it directly answers the question staging committees care about: *"Across the range of threshold probabilities at which a clinician would act differently based on this staging information, does LNR-based staging lead to better clinical decisions than N-stage-based staging?"* Calibration addresses the complementary question: *"When the model says this patient has a 30% five-year mortality risk, are roughly 30% of such patients actually dying?"* A staging system that discriminates well but calibrates poorly is clinically deceptive.

**Revision:** The performance metric hierarchy is **inverted**:

1. **Primary discrimination metric:** Uno C-statistic (more robust than Harrell's C under heavy censoring). Delta C-statistic with 95% bootstrap CI is the primary test statistic for H₁.
2. **Primary clinical utility metric:** Decision-curve analysis (DCA) — net benefit plotted across a range of threshold probabilities from 10–60% (clinically plausible range for treatment intensification decisions). This is elevated to a **co-primary performance endpoint** alongside C-statistic, reflecting its direct relevance to the staging committee audience.
3. **Calibration:** Calibration-in-the-large (mean predicted vs observed survival), calibration slope, and calibration plots (observed vs predicted survival at 3 and 5 years using 10 risk groups). Calibration is reported as a **primary secondary endpoint**.
4. **Model parsimony:** AIC and BIC retained.
5. **Reclassification:** Continuous NRI and IDI are **demoted to appendix-level secondary analyses**, clearly labelled as exploratory and subject to instability limitations. The reclassification table itself (cross-tabulation of N-stage tier × LNR tier with survival data per cell) is retained in the main results as a descriptive finding because it is clinically intuitive and directly answers the clinical question about which patients are moved to different risk categories.

### Point 7 — Chemotherapy time structure not fully resolved
**Validity: Fully valid. The original protocol acknowledged the problem but left its resolution ambiguous.**

The immortal time bias risk with adjuvant chemotherapy is concrete: patients who receive adjuvant chemotherapy must survive long enough to start it (typically 6–12 weeks post-surgery), which creates a period of immortal time that is attributed to the chemotherapy group. Including chemotherapy as a baseline yes/no variable in a Cox model starting at surgery can therefore make chemotherapy appear protective even if it is not, or distort the prognostic estimates of covariates correlated with chemotherapy receipt (including N-stage and LNR, since higher N-stage patients are more likely to receive chemotherapy).

**Revision:** Chemotherapy handling is governed by a **pre-specified decision rule** based on data availability:

- **If individual chemotherapy start dates are available:** Include as a time-varying binary covariate in all Cox models. This is the correct approach and eliminates immortal time bias.
- **If only receipt (yes/no at any point) is available without dates:** Chemotherapy is excluded from the primary OS multivariable model. It is included in a pre-specified sensitivity analysis as a fixed covariate, with explicit acknowledgement of potential immortal time bias in that sensitivity model. It is retained in the DFS multivariable model because DFS endpoint timing inherently starts at surgery and ends at recurrence, which is less susceptible to this bias.
- **In all cases:** The distribution of chemotherapy receipt across LNR tiers and N-stage categories is reported in the baseline characteristics table. If chemotherapy receipt differs substantially across groups (which is expected — N2 patients receive it more often than N1 patients), this differential is noted as a potential effect modifier.

### Point 8 — Calendar-time and institutional drift
**Validity: Valid and often underestimated in long-accrual retrospective cohorts. Partially under-acknowledged in the original protocol.**

A 2008–2022 accrual window encompasses: the pre-oxaliplatin FOLFOX era and post-FOLFOX era for adjuvant chemotherapy; changes in laparoscopic versus open surgery rates (which affects perioperative recovery, lymph node yield, and possibly long-term outcomes); the introduction of complete mesocolic excision (CME) and D3 lymphadenectomy techniques which explicitly change TLE; evolving pathological examination techniques (fat-clearing, standardised node slicing) that affect TLE; and COVID-19 related care disruption in 2020–2021. Each of these is a potential confounder of the LNR-survival relationship, and all are correlated with calendar time.

In a South Asian setting, this concern is amplified because the rate of institutional change — training, equipment, staffing — may be more rapid and less uniform than in a single stable Western centre. If multi-site recruitment is pursued, surgical and pathological variation between sites is a clustering variable that can induce apparent LNR effects that are actually site effects.

**Revision:** Calendar period is included as a covariate in all multivariable models, entered as a restricted cubic spline of year of surgery (3 knots), or as a categorical period variable (e.g. 2008–2012, 2013–2017, 2018–2022) if continuous spline fitting is impractical given sample size. If multi-site recruitment is used, a shared-frailty Cox model (with centre as the random effect) replaces the standard Cox model for all multivariable analyses. This accounts for within-centre clustering of outcomes and pathological practice. The choice between fixed-effect centre adjustment and random-effect frailty model is pre-specified based on the number of participating centres: ≤3 centres use fixed effects; ≥4 centres use shared frailty.

Additionally, TLE is plotted as a function of calendar year for descriptive purposes. A temporal trend in TLE is expected (improving node retrieval over time in most institutions) and, if confirmed, supports the necessity of adjusting for both TLE and calendar period independently.

### Point 9 — Selection bias from exclusion criteria
**Validity: Fully valid. This is a data quality and selection bias concern that affects the generalisability of findings.**

The original protocol proposed excluding patients lost to follow-up within 12 months with no death event. The critique correctly identifies that this exclusion is non-random — early loss to follow-up in a South Asian cancer cohort is likely correlated with socioeconomic factors, disease severity, performance status, and distance from the treating centre. These are all prognostically relevant variables. Excluding these patients does not remove the problem; it removes the patients who carry it, biasing the remaining cohort toward patients with better access to follow-up care. That is a systematic distortion.

The second sub-point — that excluding patients with incomplete histopathological reporting biases toward better-resourced pathology workflows — is equally valid. If TLE is missing because a specimen was inadequately processed, that missingness is informative (it is likely associated with worse pathological practice, possibly with worse outcomes), and excluding those cases removes exactly the patients where LNR's advantage over N-stage might be most apparent. This is a particularly important bias in the South Asian context where pathological practice variation is greater than in Western series.

**Revision:** The exclusion criteria are revised:

- **Patients with valid last-contact dates (even if < 12 months post-surgery) are retained and censored at that date**, not excluded. The only grounds for exclusion are: (a) no valid date of surgery, (b) no valid last-contact or death date whatsoever (truly untraceable records), or (c) confirmed data entry error rendering the record uninterpretable. This applies to fewer patients and is less likely to be systematically biased.
- **Patients with incomplete histopathological reporting (no TLE recorded)** are not excluded from the dataset. They are excluded from analyses where TLE is a required variable (Layer 2 and Layer 3 models), but retained in Layer 1 analyses (unadjusted LNR vs N-stage discrimination). In practice, if TLE is missing, LNR cannot be computed, so these patients are inherently excluded from LNR analyses — the important change is that they are **counted and characterised** rather than silently dropped. A sensitivity analysis tests whether the TLE-missing subgroup has different N-stage distribution, grade, or follow-up pattern compared to the TLE-available subgroup, which quantifies the selection bias introduced.
- A **CONSORT-style patient flow diagram** is required as a primary reporting element, showing the starting registry count, each exclusion criterion applied, the number lost at each step, and the final analytic cohort. This makes the selection process transparent and auditable.

One additional point worth noting: the critique **did not flag** the South Asian contextual dimension as a weakness, which itself is validation that this aspect of the original protocol was well-framed. The revised protocol preserves and strengthens it — the TLE distribution data, the proportion with suboptimal harvest, and the calendar-period trend in TLE adequacy are all now formal secondary outputs that constitute a standalone contribution to the international staging literature regardless of the primary LNR finding.

The revised protocol is now tighter, more defensible, and more directly aligned with what AJCC, RCPath, and ASCO reviewers will evaluate: a pre-registered, pre-specified study with a single declared primary estimand, continuous exposure modelling, calibration and decision-curve evidence alongside discrimination, and honest handling of the methodological constraints imposed by a retrospective South Asian dataset.
